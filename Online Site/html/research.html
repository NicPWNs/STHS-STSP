STSP Research Paper
When it comes to cyber-attacks, most people would picture a dark figure in a closed room hacking away into a computer terminal with lines and lines of code.  Well that’s not the case.  In fact, 66% of all cyber-attacks include the use of social engineering which is exactly the opposite of the “hacker” stereotype.  Left and right, data is being stolen; personal, private, and governmental all unsecure due to the people that created it.  In today’s age of cyber-attacks, cyber espionage, and exploitation, our weakest link lies in human error and unawareness of the cyber dimension.  This vulnerability derived from the people can be fixed by the people as well, through implications of cyber education and awareness.
So, what is social engineering? You may be asking.  Merriam-Webster defines social engineering as “the practice of making laws or using other methods to influence public opinion and solve social problems or improve social conditions” which is very different compared to its meaning in the cyber world.  When it comes to cyber-attacks, social engineering is about the methods in which an attacker uses to gain information by communicating with victim directly.  The most common of social engineering attacks include phishing which is the creation of spoofed websites and emails to be distributed in mass to random users for their data to retrieved, and also spear-phishing where the attacker specifically targets and organization or individual for a phishing attack.  Another slightly more uncommon approach is attempting to acquire information by telephone usually behind the identity of a common business or organization.  Finally, a very rare approach to social engineering includes face to face contact, where the intruder acts under another identity and attempts to talk their way into a place of limited access to perform their attacks.   Ultimately, social engineering is typically done as a preliminary action to gather information or data before performing the actual attack and technical side.  Compared to other attacks, where hours and hours of time are devoted to cracking passwords and such data, social engineering is typically favored as it is a direct route to retrieving information fast.  When related to human error, we begin to see that a large percentage of attacks occur by human error within social engineering attacks.  Usually it is simply that a subject or victim is unaware or untrained about cyber security and the way attackers may attempt to gather data and this leads to misfortunate identity theft, fraud and other issues.  Overall social engineering is prominent in the aspect of the weak human link within cyber security, and its effect on further attacks.
When it comes to seeing how these attacks work and how human awareness could have prevented them, it is best to look at a few of the many popular occurrences where user error was the issue.  In 2013 the Twitter account of the associated press reported “Breaking: Two Explosions in the White House and Barrack Obama is injured” which of course was not true.  In fact a hacking group/organization known as the Syrian Electronic Army gained access to the account and posted this information.  When relating this to human error, we must look into how they did it.  They simply sent an email to several employees at the Associated Press with an enticing yet related link that they fell for.  Upon clicking on the link, a Trojan virus took control over the machines and from there it was easy enough for the attackers to tweet!  From this we see that emails are very dangerous in this example of spear-phishing and it was the human error of the employees at the Associated Press that were not trained to not click on such links.  Another example that is much more recent is the hacking of the CIA director’s emails.  The teenager that performed the social engineering simply acted as a Verizon employee and retrived crucial personal information about him.  With this first act of social engineering completed, he then proceeded to perform another as he contacted AOL to gain access to his email account.  Using information such as his phone number and last four digits of his credit card, he convinced AOL to reset his password, giving him access to the director’s account on October 12th, 2015.  With this example we see how it is more complex than just one email that will allow access for attackers into an account, but it is a complex chain of events that did this.  Overall when comparing these two examples to the millions of attacks just like this performed daily for money, information or just fun, it really comes down to the user error and the way the user has control over their account that exposes them to these simple attacks.
That’s enough talking about the human error, now let’s get to how we can solve this immense issue that’s happening all around us and to you.  There are many educational platforms out there that are paid for but also free on both the complex business level but also on the individual level.  Programs offered by the SANS institute are highly trusted and are top of the line when it comes to protecting the largest of corporations, while programs such as Cyber Aces are simple online practices and training for the personal user and are free!  It is programs like these that need to continue to grow and become abundant so that everyone has some level of awareness on how they can protect themselves from cyber-attacks.  First we must spread awareness and our school even takes part in this as there is usually a cyber security video played on the Raven Report at least once a month.  There are also national efforts put on by the United States Department of Homeland Security such as October being the designated month for Cyber Security Awareness.  When it comes to my project, I will be created an in depth portable interface platform for users to use and learn about what is going on in the realm of cyber.  This will be an updated interface with the latest in cyber news for the user to become more aware of cyber security.
In conclusion, it is easy to see that the unaware human is main issue in cyber security.  Those that work relentlessly to secure our data are just as easily giving it away and this is endangering our country in particular.  Awareness is not going to occur by itself and therefore there must be some effort to spark the revolution in fixing the human link in cyber security.  We must stop these simple attacks with simple solutions, as the problems they create are much bigger.  Just as the speaker Albert Schultz, CEO Krypteia LLC stated “The human element is the most vulnerable,” I agree, and I would also say that we can fix it.
